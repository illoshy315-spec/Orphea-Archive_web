Salem Witch Trials

Record 01 · The Core Mechanism

The crime you could never disprove

"I saw your ghost strangle me in my sleep." Try building a defense against that.

The Salem court accepted spectral evidence — testimony that the accused's spirit, not their body, had appeared and tormented the witness. An alibi was worthless: you could be sound asleep in your own bed and still be convicted of an assault your specter committed elsewhere.

There was no way to rebut it. The accusation itself was the proof, and it was permitted precisely because it was unfalsifiable — a courtroom built around a claim no defendant could ever disprove.

The instinct

Bring an alibi. Prove where you actually were.

The record

Alibis were irrelevant. Only your specter was on trial.

Record 02 · Trial by Ordeal

Touch her. If she stops screaming, you did it.

There was no version of this test you could pass.

When an "afflicted" girl fell into convulsions in open court, the accused could be ordered to touch her. The theory: a witch's power flows back into its owner on contact, ending the fit.

So if the screaming stopped the moment you touched her — that was proof. And if you refused to touch her at all — that was proof too, of a witch too afraid to be caught. The test wasn't designed to find an answer. It was designed to always return the same one.

The instinct

Refuse. Don't participate in a rigged demonstration.

The record

Refusal was read as a witch protecting her power.

Record 03 · The Central Irony

Confessing was the only thing that saved you

Nineteen people told the truth. All nineteen hanged.

Every person who maintained their innocence to the end of the Salem trials was executed — nineteen people, all of them. Every person who confessed survived. Not one confessor was put to death.

A false confession bought you time, credibility with the court, and — critically — a way out: naming others as accomplices. Telling the truth bought you a rope. The court had built a system where honesty was the single most dangerous thing you could offer it.

The instinct

Innocent people should assert their innocence.

The record

Asserting innocence was the only guaranteed path to the gallows.

Record 04 · A Trap for the Reasonable

Calling out the liars got you hanged faster

"She's obviously faking it" was the last logical thing some people ever said.

To a modern eye, the girls' convulsions look staged — timed to eye contact, escalating with an audience. Several defendants said exactly that out loud in court.

It did not go well for them. Publicly accusing the afflicted girls of lying was read as a direct assault on the court's own evidence — and by extension, on the girls themselves. It escalated a defendant from "suspected" to "confirmed" faster than almost anything else a person could do.

The instinct

Point out the obvious performance. Logic wins.

The record

Contradicting the girls was treated as an attack on the court.

Record 05 · A Debated Theory

Maybe it wasn't witchcraft. Maybe it was breakfast.

One scientist's theory: the "witches" and their accusers may have shared a fungus.

In 1976, behavioral scientist Linda Caporael proposed that convulsions, hallucinations, and skin sensations reported in Salem may have been symptoms of ergotism — poisoning from a fungus (Claviceps purpurea) that grows on damp rye, a staple grain in the colony's diet that season.

This is a hypothesis, not a settled fact. Historians have raised real objections — the symptom match is imperfect, and the outbreak's social pattern (accusations clustering along existing land and family disputes) is better explained by politics than biology. We include it here because it's a real, published theory — not because Orphea has ruled on which is true.

⚠ contestedLinda Caporael, "Ergotism: The Satan Loosed in Salem," Science, 1976. Widely cited and widely disputed.

Record 06 · Who Actually Got Accused

The more land you owned, the more likely you were a witch

A guilty verdict could turn your farm into the county's farm.

A conviction could result in the Crown seizing the condemned's property. Landowners — especially women who'd inherited or would inherit land, which put them outside the normal male chain of property control — were disproportionately represented among the accused.

Presenting a legal deed as proof of your good standing could backfire in the opposite direction: it told the court exactly how much you owned, and exactly how much there was to gain from your conviction.

The instinct

Produce the paperwork. Documentation proves you're legitimate.

The record

Documentation proved you were worth accusing.

✓ verifiedPattern documented across multiple estate-seizure cases, including Giles Corey's.

Record 07 · The Third Option

Refuse to plead, and they add weight. Literally.

Giles Corey's last words were an instruction to his executioners: "More weight."

A defendant who refused to enter any plea couldn't legally be tried — so the court applied peine forte et dure: the accused was laid down and had stones piled onto their chest, more added each day, until they entered a plea or died.

Giles Corey chose silence for two days, and it worked, in the narrowest possible sense: a never-tried man's property couldn't be legally seized, so his sons kept the farm. He died to protect an inheritance, not a principle the court would ever have recognized.

The instinct

An unfair court has no right to try me. I won't participate.

The record

Non-participation had its own, physically literal, penalty.

✓ verifiedLibrary of Congress, "The Crushing Death of Giles Corey of Salem, 1692."

Record 08 · A Delay, Not a Pardon

Pregnancy bought you time. Not your life.

The court would wait for the baby. It would not spare the mother.

A pregnant woman under sentence of death received a stay of execution until after she gave birth — English common law didn't permit executing a woman carrying a child who had committed no crime of her own.

Elizabeth Proctor was convicted and sentenced to hang. Her pregnancy delayed the execution long enough that the trials collapsed before her date arrived, and she survived — but the stay itself was never a pardon. It was a postponement with an expiration date.

✓ verifiedHistory.com, "Salem Witch Trials."
