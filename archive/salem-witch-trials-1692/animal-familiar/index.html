Instance 01 — Massachusetts Bay, 1692
Record 05 · Redirecting Suspicion

Report your neighbor's cat, and it's a perfectly normal Tuesday

Naming an animal as a witch's accomplice was standard practice. It didn't end with the animal.

A familiar, in the theology behind the trials, was an imp or minor demon a witch kept as a servant — most often described in the shape of a cat, dog, or bird. Reporting a neighbor's animal as suspicious wasn't a fringe accusation; it was one of the most ordinary moves available to a frightened colonist, requiring no confrontation with a person at all.

Tituba, the first person to confess in the outbreak, gave courtroom testimony describing a red cat, a black dog, and a yellow bird as agents of the Devil who had pressured her into service. Her testimony was treated as credible, detailed evidence — not folklore, but a witness statement.

A widely retold account from Andover holds that an afflicted girl accused a neighbor's dog of tormenting her, and villagers shot the animal on the spot. Only afterward did Cotton Mather reason the dog had likely been innocent all along — if it had truly been Satan in disguise, he argued, it couldn't have died so easily. One of the only acquittals the entire episode produced, and it went to a dog, posthumously. (This specific anecdote isn't part of the surviving official court record — it's filed here as a well-documented historical account, not a courtroom fact, which is why this record is marked verifying rather than verified.)

The trap for a modern instinct is that naming an animal feels like a victimless move — no person is directly harmed, and you've demonstrated vigilance to a watching community. But a familiar implies an owner. The accusation was never really about the animal; it was a slower-burning way of pointing at whoever the animal belonged to, and the community rarely let it rest there for long.

Orphea's Note

The cat was never questioned. It still lost.
