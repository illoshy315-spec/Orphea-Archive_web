Instance 01 — Massachusetts Bay, 1692
Record 05 · Redirecting Suspicion

Report your neighbor's cat, and it's a perfectly normal Tuesday

Naming an animal as a witch's accomplice was standard practice. It didn't end with the animal.

The Mechanism

A familiar, in the theology behind the trials, was an imp or minor demon a witch kept as a servant — most often described in the shape of a cat, dog, or bird. Reporting a neighbor's animal as suspicious wasn't a fringe accusation; it was one of the most ordinary moves available to a frightened colonist — and a deceptively safe-feeling one, since no person had to be confronted directly. But a familiar implies an owner. The accusation was never really about the animal; it was a slower-burning way of pointing at whoever it belonged to, and the community rarely let it rest there for long.

Tituba, the first person to confess in the outbreak, gave courtroom testimony describing a red cat, a black dog, and a yellow bird as agents of the Devil who had pressured her into service. Her testimony was treated as credible, detailed evidence — not folklore, but a witness statement.

Case on File — Andover

A widely retold account from Andover holds that an afflicted girl accused a neighbor's dog of tormenting her, and villagers shot the animal on the spot. Only afterward did Cotton Mather reason the dog had likely been innocent all along — if it had truly been Satan in disguise, he argued, it couldn't have died so easily. One of the only acquittals the entire episode produced, and it went to a dog, posthumously. (This specific anecdote isn't part of the surviving official court record — it's filed here as a well-documented historical account, not a courtroom fact, which is why this record is marked verifying rather than verified.)

The Echo

A version of this same reflex resurfaced in the United States three centuries later, during the ‘Satanic Panic’ of the 1980s and early 1990s. Day-care abuse cases across the country came to include claims that caregivers had involved animals in rituals — in one Southern California case, children accused a defendant of killing a giraffe and an elephant in front of them. A 1994 survey of more than 11,000 psychiatric and police professionals nationwide, conducted for the federal government's National Center on Child Abuse and Neglect, found no substantiated evidence of the large-scale, organized ritual-abuse rings the panic assumed — but by then, dozens of prosecutions had already been built on testimony no more verifiable than Tituba's yellow bird.

Orphea's Note

The cat was never questioned. It still lost.
